Artificial intelligence to tackle climate change

Climate change affects human health, agriculture, economy, ecosystem, and biodiversity, ultimately hampering human sustainability. The major climate problem that may be questioning human existence on the earth is sea-level rise, global warming, failure of the ecosystem, extreme weather, and natural disaster. Artificial intelligence here able to tackle uncontrolled climate change to make our planet more liveable. The principal aim of the project is to adapt and mitigate climate changes more robustly with AI by tracking the climate change factor, such as greenhouse gas emission. Also, Al will make early predictions of climate consequences such as floods, drought, hurricanes, cyclones, ice sheet melting, sea-level rise, global warming, and heatwaves so that the corresponding authority can take early steps to tackle them.

Machine learning to predict the seasonal weather incorporating the ENSO condition
  • Warmer or colder than average ocean temperature in one part of the world can influence weather around the globe. During the normal condition, trade wind which blows from east to west pushes warm surface waters towards Asia. But in some year when the trade wind becomes weaker, the warm surface water moves eastward and reduce upwelling of cold water off the coast of South America. Climatologist called is El Niño. Its climate impact shows up mostly in the wintertime over North America. The warmer ocean fuels an intensification and southward shift of the jet stream which results in flooding in the southern United States and warmer, drier conditions over the parts of the pacific northwest, Canada and the northern US. But eventually, those trade wind pick up again and sometimes become more robust than the normal. When that happens, they blow the warmer water back into the western pacific and restart the upwelling of cold water towards the surface in the eastern pacific. This strong trade wind causes La Niña, unusually cold conditions in the tropical pacific. The cooling phase La Niña can lead to drought in the southern part of the US and cooler temperature, heavy rains and flooding in the pacific northwest. El Niño and La Nina are part of a cycle that influence extreme weather and impact food production, water supply, and human health in many parts of the globe. ENSO cycle is a scientific term that describes the fluctuations in temperature between the ocean and atmosphere in the east-central equatorial Pacific. These deviations from average sea surface temperatures (SSTs) can have large-scale impacts on ocean processes, global weather, and climate. El Niño and La Niña are opposite phases of what is known as the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) cycle. El Niño and La Niña are complex weather patterns resulting from variations in ocean temperatures in the Equatorial Pacific. La Niña is sometimes referred to as the cold phase of ENSO and El Niño as the warm phase of ENSO. These deviations from average surface temperatures can have large-scale impacts not only on ocean processes but also on global weather and climate. Our aim of the research is to predict the seasonal weather incorporating the ENSO condition.
